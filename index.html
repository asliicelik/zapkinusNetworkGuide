LAYER 1

Cable/Port Issue

Problem

My computer starts up without any problems, but I can't see any network connection, and it won't connect to the internet. My friends are waiting for me in the game lobby, so I start troubleshooting. Suspecting the issue might be with the cable, I check the Ethernet cable and notice that the end of it is crushed. I guess my mom accidentally stepped on it or damaged it while cleaning, without noticing.

Solution

I replaced the damaged Ethernet cable with a new one. After plugging in the new cable, the network connection was restored, and I was able to access the internet again.

PC Network Adapter Off

Problem

During my internship at Halkbank, I connect the laptop given to me to the Ethernet cable at the company. However, I cannot access the internet. When I contact the IT department, they confirm that the cable is working, the switch port is active and the connection is physically strong. However, my computer still cannot connect to the network.

Solution

SI went to Control Panel > Network and Sharing Center > Change Adapter Settings, right-clicked on the network adapter and selected "Enable". After this process, the computer was able to use the network hardware again and the internet connection was established.

VLAN Misconfiguration

Problem

The other day, a friend of mine from EEE department asked me for help because their newly installed computer couldn't connect to an old server in their lab. They said both devices were connected to the same switch, and the internet was working fine, but no matter what they tried, the new PC just couldn't reach the server ‚Äî not even a ping.

Solution

I offered to take a quick look, and when I checked the switch configuration, I noticed the server was assigned to VLAN 20 while the new PC was in VLAN 10. That's when I explained to them that even though both devices were plugged into the same switch, VLANs create separate virtual networks, so their data can't reach each other without proper routing.

LAYER 2

GSBWiFi: Single Device Restriction via MAC-Based Access

Problem

I am currently staying in a government dormitory reported that I could only connect one device to the Wi-Fi network at a time. After logging in with my credentials on my phone, trying to connect with my PC would result in either a redirect loop or full denial of access. The strange part? Even though my both devices used the same username and password, only the first device could use the network.

Solution

Upon closer analysis, it became evident that the dormitory network uses MAC address filtering at Layer 2, most likely combined with sticky port security on access switches or MAC-based RADIUS authentication. Once a user logs in, their MAC address is associated with their credentials, and only that MAC is whitelisted for future access. This prevents account sharing and enforces a one-device-per-user policy.

Other Players Cannot Join the Lobby Due to Player Connecting to the Game via VPN

Problem

I was talking to my friend who is currently an intern at a gaming company recently and he told me the following: "We are currently working on a game using the host client approach. If one of the players connects to the game using a VPN, the other player cannot connect to the lobby. While researching how to solve this, I remembered my networking class."

Solution

To solve this problem, I designed a Cisco-based network to show why VPN users could not theoretically establish a printer. It was necessary to detect that the VPN output behind NAT was forced to provide a direct connection and that this could be due to lack of port forwarding or access control lists (ACL). As a solution, it was configured to allow configuration information with port forwarding settings and ACL over NAT. This scenario aims to solve the problems of a player with VPN in a host-client gaming architecture at the network level.

LAYER 3

Different Network Protocol Incompatibility

Problem

A company has two different office networks: Accounting and Administration Office The company's DHCP server is only located in the Accounting Office. However, when a new employee connects his computer to the Administration Office, the device cannot access the Internet and cannot obtain an IP address.

Solution

Since the DHCP server was located in a different network, DHCP requests from clients could not reach this server. To solve this problem, the IP address of the DHCP server was defined on the router interface on the client's network using the ip helper-address command. Thus, the router started to forward DHCP Discover packets as unicast to the DHCP server and the client was able to automatically obtain an IP address.

LAYER 4

Dormitory Printer Crashed Due to UDP Flood

Problem

While staying in a government dormitory last year, we had a shared network printer connected via Ethernet through a central switch. All the students in the dorm could send print jobs to it from their rooms over the internal network. Everything worked fine‚Äîuntil one day the printer stopped responding completely. At first, I thought it was just a random printer error. But after talking to a few friends, I realized no one was able to print anything. The printer wasn‚Äôt even acknowledging jobs. Eventually, a dorm technician came in and had to restart the printer manually to get it working again.

Solution

Later, I discovered that the issue might have been caused by one of the students unknowingly downloading a modded game file that included hidden malware. The infected device began flooding the printer with random UDP packets. Since many printers listen on open UDP ports (like 161 for SNMP or 9100 for raw printing), this flood overwhelmed the printer‚Äôs network interface, causing it to freeze or crash entirely. This experience taught me how easily a simple UDP flood could bring down a critical shared device in a local network‚Äîand how something as innocent as a game mod could be a vehicle for a denial-of-service incident.

LAYER 6

Wi-Fi Encryption Incompatibility

Problem

The other day I went to a friend's house with my laptop. I wanted to use the internet connection at his house and connected to the Wi-Fi network without any problems. However, when my friend turned on his old computer and wanted to connect to the internet, the computer could not see the Wi-Fi network or kept giving an error when trying to connect.

Solution

In this scenario, the encryption mismatch issue my friend experienced is resolved by downgrading the router's wireless security settings to a protocol that the old device also supports, or by configuring it in a mixed mode. In real life, this usually means downgrading the Wi-Fi encryption type from WPA2 to WPA/WPA2 mixed mode, or in rare cases, to WPA.
